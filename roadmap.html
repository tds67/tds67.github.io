What should the region do?

Below is a practical roadmap that a government, university project, or organization can adapt. It focuses on governance, workforce, and technical controls — plus clear ways to measure progress.

Principle: protect the highest‑value services first (identity, email, finance, energy, citizen portals), while raising a minimum baseline everywhere.

Phase 1 — 0 to 6 months: raise the baseline fast

A. Create clear cybersecurity ownership

Governance
  • Appoint a security lead per ministry/agency and a coordinating central function.
  • Define who can declare an incident, who communicates publicly, and who can approve emergency actions.

B. Strengthen incident response coordination (CSIRT/“CERT”)

Readiness

Iraq has a national incident response function (often referenced as IQ‑CERT). The Kurdistan Region can strengthen coordination with it and/or formalize a dedicated regional capability for faster local response. [S13] [S8]

  • Publish a single reporting channel for government incidents.
  • Build playbooks: phishing outbreak, ransomware, data leak, DDoS, compromised admin account.

C. Lock down identity & email first

High leverage
  • Mandatory MFA for administrators and remote access; then expand MFA to all staff.
  • Harden email: DMARC/SPF/DKIM, phishing reporting button, and modern authentication.
  • Privilege review: remove unused admin accounts; “least privilege” roles.

D. Basic hygiene program

Baseline
  • Asset inventory: know what exists and who owns it.
  • Patch cadence: internet-facing systems patched quickly; endpoints monthly at minimum.
  • Backups: offline/immutable backups + regular restore drills.
  • Central logging for critical systems + clear retention policy.

Phase 2 — 6 to 18 months: build resilience and trust

1) Adopt a maturity framework

Pick one common language (NIST CSF or ISO 27001) for targets, audits, and investment decisions.

  • Define “minimum controls” for all agencies.
  • Define “enhanced controls” for high‑risk services (finance, energy, identity systems).

2) Protect critical infrastructure

Separate IT from OT where possible, control vendor access, and practice outage response.

  • Network segmentation and strict remote access to industrial systems.
  • Tabletop exercises with energy/telecom providers and emergency teams.

3) Secure procurement & vendors

Most breaches enter through misconfigurations or third parties. Make security part of contracts.

  • Security requirements in RFPs and renewals (MFA, logging, incident notification).
  • Third‑party risk reviews for major suppliers.

4) Workforce development

Build pipelines: scholarships, internships, labs, and joint programs with universities and industry.

  • Cyber ranges / practical labs.
  • Clear career paths (SOC analyst → engineer → incident responder → architect).

Phase 3 — 18 to 36 months: advanced capability

A. Continuous monitoring (SOC) and threat intelligence

Capability

Targeted campaigns described by threat research show the need for detection and response, not just prevention. [S4]

  • Central SOC for government networks and critical services.
  • Threat intel sharing with trusted partners and regional peers.

B. Modern, rights‑respecting cybercrime legislation

Policy
  • Clear definitions and proportional penalties focused on real harm (fraud, extortion, intrusion).
  • Judicial safeguards and transparency to maintain public trust. [S6]

C. Public awareness at scale

Society
  • Regular campaigns on phishing and scams; easy reporting channels.
  • Security in schools/universities (digital literacy + basic cyber hygiene).

Metrics you can track

Time-to-patch

How fast are critical vulnerabilities fixed on internet-facing systems?

MFA coverage

% of staff and 100% of admins protected with MFA.

Incident response

Mean time to detect (MTTD) and mean time to recover (MTTR).
