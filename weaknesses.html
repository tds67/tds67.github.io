Current weaknesses and why they matter

Advancements in digital services are real — but attackers also adapt quickly. Below are major weakness areas that typically drive the highest real‑world impact (service disruption, data exposure, fraud, and loss of trust).

Important: “Weakness” doesn’t mean “failure.” It means where risk concentrates today and where improvements buy down the most risk per dollar.

1) Targeted cyber‑espionage against Kurdish and Iraqi officials

High impact

Independent threat research describes long-running, Iran-aligned cyber‑espionage activity targeting Kurdish and Iraqi government officials, including compromises inside Kurdistan Regional Government environments. [S4]

  • Why it matters: espionage can leak diplomatic, security, and energy‑sector information.
  • What it exploits: exposed services, weak identity controls, and lack of detection.

2) Uneven security maturity across institutions

Common

Digital transformation often progresses faster than security governance and enforcement. Reporting on Iraq’s cyber environment emphasizes that having a strategy is not enough without implementation and institutional capacity. [S11]

  • Different ministries (and state-owned enterprises) may run different tools, standards, and practices.
  • Security teams may be under‑resourced compared to the growth of systems they must defend.

3) Legal and policy gaps (and “overbroad” draft approaches)

Governance

Civil society analysis of Iraq’s cybercrime / information crime draft legislation highlights the risk of overly broad provisions and rights impacts, alongside the real need to address cyber‑enabled crime. [S6] [S7]

  • Risk: laws that are vague can harm trust and innovation, and still fail to improve security.
  • Need: clear definitions, due process, and strong capacity for investigation of real cybercrime.

4) Critical infrastructure exposure (energy, finance, telecom)

Systemic

As the region’s economy relies on energy and digital services, outages or compromises can cascade. National-level cybersecurity planning exists, but translating policy into resilient operations is a long journey. [S5]

  • Operational technology (OT) and industrial systems often lag behind in patching and segmentation.
  • Supply-chain and vendor access can become a weak link if not controlled.

5) Public awareness & cyber-enabled fraud

Everyday risk

As services move online, citizens and businesses face increased exposure to phishing, scams, and account takeovers. Improving user safety requires both education and safer-by-default systems (MFA, alerts, recovery).

  • Phishing and credential reuse remain the fastest path into accounts.
  • Underreporting makes it harder to measure the scale of fraud and improve defenses.

So what should be prioritized?

The most effective approach is a balanced plan: raise baseline controls everywhere, then add specialized capabilities for high‑risk targets. The next page proposes a practical roadmap tailored to the Kurdistan Region’s situation.
